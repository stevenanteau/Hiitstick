Activity 2 · Build a compass

Your Compass

A compass has two needles: a Workview (what work is for) and a Lifeview (what life is for). Written down and read together, they help you tell whether a path is heading “true north” — even when you don't yet know the destination.

Why do this?

Big decisions get easier when you know what you actually believe. Most of us have never put our philosophy of work or life into words — so we drift by other people's definitions of success. Writing these two short reflections gives you a reference point you can hold any opportunity up against. The goal isn't a perfect essay; it's an honest one.

A tip: write about why, not what. Not “I want to be a nurse,” but “work matters to me because…”. Aim for a paragraph or two each — around 250 words is plenty.

1 · Your Workview

Let these questions pull the writing out of you:
  • Why work at all? What's work for?
  • What does work mean to you — and how does it serve others and society?
  • What makes work good or worthwhile?
  • What does money have to do with it?
  • How do experience, growth, and fulfillment fit in?

A snippet, to show the tone:

“I believe work is how I turn my attention into something useful for other people. Money matters, but as fuel, not as the scoreboard. Good work stretches me a little and leaves the people around me better than it found them…”
0 words
✦

2 · Your Lifeview

And these for the bigger picture:
  • Why are we here? What's the meaning or purpose of life?
  • What's the relationship between you and others — family, community, world?
  • What is good, and what is evil?
  • Is there a higher power, or something transcendent?
  • What's the role of joy, sorrow, justice, love, and peace?

Again, just the tone:

“I think life is mostly about paying attention and being kind while I'm here. Meaning isn't handed to you — you make it, in small acts, with the people you love. Sorrow is the price of caring, and worth paying…”
0 words
✦

3 · Read them together

Now hold both up to the light. A good compass is coherent — your work and life views point roughly the same way.
